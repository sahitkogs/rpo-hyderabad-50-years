# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

**Primary: MEA and officialdom.** Senior officers at the Regional Passport Office, the Ministry of External Affairs, sister RPOs across India, ministerial reviewers, and journalists writing the institutional story. They open the site to assess whether the 50-year milestone has been marked with the gravity it deserves. Their bar is institutional standard, not citizen ease.

**Secondary: Telugu-speaking citizens, mobile-first.** Applicants who land here looking for FAQ videos, walk-in hours, contact details. They will spend less time on the page than the primary audience, but they are the moral reason the office exists, and the design must visibly serve them — not as an afterthought.

**Tertiary: Press, alumni, civic public.** Journalists covering the anniversary, retired RPO Hyderabad staff, civic-minded readers. They want the narrative — the 1976 origin, the recent reforms, the national award.

The job to be done: read the office's 50-year story, find proof of citizen-centric reform, judge the institution by what it has chosen to put on this page.

## Product Purpose

A single-page commemorative microsite marking 50 years of service by the Regional Passport Office, Hyderabad (1976–2026). It exists to formalize the milestone publicly, document the office's recent reforms with credible sourcing, and give Telugu-speaking citizens a clear path to the FAQ video library and contact channels.

It is explicitly not a service portal. All applications and tracking go to passportindia.gov.in. The site's success is measured by how the institution is perceived after a careful read — does it feel like an office that takes its work, its citizens, and its 50-year history seriously.

## Brand Personality

**Ceremonial. Archival. Considered.**

The voice is a senior officer addressing the public on a state occasion — measured cadence, respectful of the moment, never breathless. The visual language is closer to a commemorative stamp release or a museum exhibition catalogue than to a website. Festivity is expressed through gravity and craft, not through banners or party energy.

Warmth is present but earned through specifics — real names, real numbers, real photographs — rather than through smiling stock people or friendly copy.

## Anti-references

- **Generic stock photos of "happy citizens".** No diverse-smiling-people-holding-passports imagery. No handshake metaphors. No trust-bank visual language. Use real photographs of real people, with consent, or no photograph.
- **The default sarkari-template look.** Dense top nav, scrolling tickers, clip-art icons, mismatched typography, decade-old layouts. Every reflexive choice that lands the design at "looks like a state portal" is the wrong choice. Editorial standards apply throughout.
- **Personal-showcase framing of the RPO.** The office is the protagonist, not any individual. Avoid headline portraits, centered-leadership layouts, or any composition that reads as a profile of one officer.
- **Em dashes in copy.** Use commas, colons, semicolons, periods, or parentheses.

Independence-Day-banner kitsch and SaaS-dashboard polish are not hard bans, but treat them as failure modes — civic motifs only when they serve the ceremony, never as decoration; modern polish only in service of clarity, never as the look itself.

## Design Principles

1. **Institutional first, personal second.** The office is fifty years old; the current officer has held charge for two. The composition reflects that. Leadership appears at meaningful turns — the 2023 milestone, the message section, a signed footer — not as a feature.

2. **Ceremony, not celebration.** Civic festivity expressed through state-occasion weight: commemorative typography, considered cadence, archival color. The energy of a Republic Day ceremony, not a party.

3. **Earn the gravitas with truth.** The reforms are real and well-sourced (see Section 11 of the project plan). Lean on the actual numbers, the actual award, the actual photographs. Specificity is the antidote to government-website skepticism.

4. **Reject the template default.** Every choice that would land the design at "looks like every other state portal" is the wrong choice. When in doubt, hold the editorial standard a museum catalogue would.

5. **Mobile-first without lowering craft.** The citizen audience reads on phones. That is a reason to invest more in Telugu typography, touch targets, and contrast — not less. Mobile is not the cheaper canvas.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- **WCAG 2.1 AA target** for a government-adjacent site. Contrast, focus states, semantic markup, keyboard navigation are not optional.
- **Telugu typography is a first-class concern.** Test Noto Sans Telugu or Mandali at body and headline sizes. Latin-first scales routinely break Telugu rendering — verify rather than assume.
- **Mobile-first.** Most citizen visits will be on a phone. All section layouts must hold up at 360px width.
- **Reduced motion.** Honor `prefers-reduced-motion`. The ceremonial register does not depend on motion to do its work.
- **No data collection of any kind.** No forms, no analytics that fingerprint citizens, no third-party trackers. The site collects nothing about its visitors. This is a deliberate choice and part of how the institution earns trust.
